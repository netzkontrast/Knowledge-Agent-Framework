# **Strategic Communications and Marketing in DACH Economic Research Institutes: Practices, Paradigms, and AI Integration (2025/2026)**

## **Executive Summary**

The operational landscape of economic research institutes and policy think tanks within the DACH region (Germany, Austria, Switzerland) functions at a highly complex intersection of rigorous empirical inquiry, dynamic public discourse, and targeted political advocacy. Operating within the 2025/2026 macroeconomic environment—a period defined by structural transformations, energy transition pressures, and geopolitical volatility—these institutions face unprecedented demands for real-time, evidence-based policy counsel. This exhaustive research report analyzes the prevailing standards and methodologies in marketing, communications, and *Wissenschaftskommunikation* (science communication) deployed by the leading economic institutes in this ecosystem.  
A central finding of this analysis is that generic corporate marketing methodologies are fundamentally incompatible with the institutional imperatives of DACH research bodies. Instead, leading entities such as the ifo Institut, the DIW Berlin, and the Institut der deutschen Wirtschaft (IW Köln) deploy highly specialized communication architectures. These structures are defined by signature publication formats, such as the *ifo Schnelldienst* or the *DIW Wochenbericht*, choreographed media relations governed by strict embargo (*Sperrfrist*) practices, and a nuanced approach to stakeholder engagement that strictly complies with increasingly rigorous transparency and *Lobbyregister* regulations.1 Furthermore, digital content strategies have matured significantly, pivoting away from broad social media dispersion toward highly targeted business-to-policy (B2P) engagement, predominantly localized on LinkedIn. Concurrently, interactive data visualizations have emerged as a paramount asset class for public engagement, translating abstract econometrics into tangible, personalized user experiences.  
Crucially, the 2025/2026 timeframe is defined by the rapid institutionalization of generative Artificial Intelligence within the editorial workflows of these think tanks. The integration of AI is heavily governed by recent sector-specific frameworks, most notably the May 2025 \#FactoryWisskomm Taskforce recommendations and the March 2026 guidelines published by the Bundesverband Hochschulkommunikation.3 These frameworks emphasize the necessity of the "human in the loop," strict data privacy boundaries under the DSGVO, and the absolute preservation of scientific neutrality. This report synthesizes these operational, technological, and ethical paradigms to construct a foundational matrix. This matrix is designed specifically to configure a German-language AI advisor agent tailored for the communications team at the IW Köln, ensuring that the deployment of artificial intelligence respects the sector's rigorous conventions while maximizing public and political leverage.

## **1\. THE PEER LANDSCAPE**

The DACH economic research sector is dominated by a constellation of highly influential institutes, each possessing a distinct mandate, ideological orientation, and communications operational model. Understanding this landscape requires differentiating between institutes that strictly adhere to neutral-academic mandates, those with explicit socio-political alignments (such as employer versus union affiliations), and those operating as formalized governmental advisory bodies. The communications strategies deployed by these entities are inextricably linked to their institutional identities.

### **Profiling the Major DACH Economic Institutes**

The **ifo Institut** (München), which celebrated its 75th anniversary in 2024, stands as arguably the most visible economic think tank in Germany, maintaining a commanding media presence.1 Operating with a predominantly neutral-academic yet broadly market-oriented alignment, its communications strategy is relentlessly data-driven and high-volume. The institute issued 261 press releases and participated in 380 interviews in 2024 alone, effectively dictating the national economic news cycle.1 Its signature formats include the *ifo Geschäftsklimaindex*—the premier leading indicator for the German economy—and the *ifo Schnelldienst*, a publication format established in 1948\.1 The institute's digital dominance is anchored by executive thought leadership; President Clemens Fuest's positioning as a "LinkedIn Top Voice" with over 65,200 followers exemplifies the institutional strategy of leveraging leadership profiles to bypass traditional media gatekeepers.1  
Positioned in the political epicenter of Berlin, the **DIW Berlin** (Deutsches Institut für Wirtschaftsforschung) operates with a broadly progressive, neutral-academic orientation. The institute's communication frequently emphasizes structural inequality, gender economics, and the socio-ecological transformation.2 The core of its communications output is the *DIW Wochenbericht*, a highly respected format that translates complex modeling into digestible policy insights. Recent editions have covered the climate impact of high-income households, the efficacy of the "Worst First" building renovation policy, and the economic drivers behind populist voting behavior.2 The institute supplements this with *DIW aktuell* for rapid-response commentary and highly visible video formats like "Nachgeforscht".2 The DIW excels in highly personalized science communication, leveraging the strong media profiles of its department heads to secure high resonance in national debates.  
The **RWI – Leibniz-Institut für Wirtschaftsforschung** (Essen) maintains a rigorous empirical and econometric focus. Its communications are heavily tethered to proprietary datasets and cyclical indices. A prime example is the *RWI/ISL-Containerumschlag-Index*, which serves as a global barometer for international trade and is routinely cited in global financial media, particularly during geopolitical supply chain disruptions.5 The tone of RWI is highly academic, cautious, and neutral, often acting as a stabilizing, data-first voice against speculative economic narratives in the press.  
Operating with an explicit structural alignment, the **IMK** (Institut für Makroökonomie und Konjunkturforschung) is housed within the union-affiliated Hans-Böckler-Stiftung.6 Consequently, its tone and framing consistently prioritize aggregate demand, wage dynamics, and purchasing power. Signature formats include the *Inflationsmonitor* and various *Policy Briefs* focusing on social inequality and the distributional impacts of economic shocks.7 The communication strategy frequently frames inflation and energy crises through the lens of social justice—for instance, analyzing how fuel prices disproportionately affect lower-income demographics—contrasting sharply with the competitiveness-focused narratives of employer-aligned institutes.7  
Conversely, the **IW Köln** (Institut der deutschen Wirtschaft) is explicitly employer- and business-aligned. The institute describes itself as standing for a "socially responsible free market economy".8 Its communications are meticulously designed to highlight location competitiveness (*Standortwettbewerb*), crisis resilience (*Krisenresilienz*), and the structural conditions required for industrial transformation.9 A distinct strength of the IW is its sophisticated use of digital interactivity to personalize macroeconomic data. The institute recognized early that static charts are insufficient for modern engagement; interactive graphics invite users to participate, creating "Aha-Erlebnisse" (eureka moments) that remain in the public memory longer than text alone.9 In terms of overarching media resonance, the IW frequently shares the top tier of influence with the ifo Institut, dictating the employer-side narrative in national media.1  
The **Sachverständigenrat zur Begutachtung der gesamtwirtschaftlichen Entwicklung** (SVR) occupies a unique position. As the official advisory council to the federal government, the SVR is not a traditional think tank but a statutory body. Its communication is highly choreographed and defined by institutional gravitas. The centerpiece is the massive *Jahresgutachten*, delivered annually.10 The SVR's communication is characterized by strict embargo periods and a formalized *Schweigeperiode* (period of silence), during which council members refrain from public commentary to preserve the market and political impact of the report's official release.11  
Other critical players include the **Kiel Institut für Weltwirtschaft** (IfW), which focuses heavily on global macroeconomics, trade policy, and geopolitical economic impacts.12 The **IWH** (Halle) directs its communications toward structural change, productivity dynamics, and financial stability, often with a regional emphasis on Eastern Germany.13 The **ZEW** (Mannheim) emphasizes microeconometric analysis and European integration, relying heavily on its *ZEW Finanzmarkttest* (Financial Market Survey) to gauge investor sentiment, utilizing highly targeted financial media relations to disseminate its findings.14 Finally, the **Bertelsmann Stiftung** operates as a massive, privately funded foundation with a broad socio-economic mandate, utilizing extensive financial resources to produce highly polished, multi-channel reports that often bridge the gap between economic research and societal trend analysis.15

### **Tone and Framing: Alignment Dynamics**

The fundamental distinction between institutes like the IW Köln, the IMK, and neutral bodies like ifo or DIW lies in their epistemic framing and tonal execution.  
For employer-aligned institutes like the IW, the tone is inherently pragmatic, solutions-oriented, and frequently emphasizes supply-side economics. Framing centers on reducing bureaucracy (*Bürokratieabbau*), securing energy competitiveness, and highlighting the systemic burden of high corporate taxation and non-wage labor costs.12 Conversely, union-aligned institutes like the IMK deploy a tone that is protective and socially analytical. Their framing centers on demand-side economics, emphasizing the necessity of state investment, the protection of real wages, and the risks of fiscal austerity, such as their vocal opposition to strict interpretations of the *Schuldenbremse* during economic downturns.7  
Neutral-academic institutes (ifo, DIW, RWI) strive for objective detachment, though institutional "house views" often emerge organically from their chosen research focuses. Their framing is heavily dependent on the methodological approach of specific studies, often navigating complex trade-offs—for example, balancing ambitious climate action targets with necessary social compensation mechanisms to maintain public support.2

## **2\. WISSENSCHAFTSKOMMUNIKATION (Science Communication)**

*Wissenschaftskommunikation* in the DACH region has evolved from a secondary, administrative function into a highly professionalized, distinct operational discipline. The 2025/2026 landscape requires institutes to bridge the formidable gap between mathematically dense econometrics and public accessibility, without ever sacrificing empirical fidelity. The stakes are immense; in an era marked by "Verunsicherung und Bedrohung" (uncertainty and threat), the integrity of the scientific discourse is a vital democratic asset.16

### **The "Übersetzungs"-Discipline**

Translating comprehensive, multi-hundred-page studies (*Gutachten*) into public-ready formats requires a specialized editorial process often referred to as the translation or *Übersetzungs*\-discipline. The objective is to make advanced econometrics accessible without introducing distortion. This involves distilling the core mechanism of a study—moving beyond the methodology to highlight the causal relationships. For instance, translating a vector autoregression model detailing the pass-through effects of energy prices into a clear, narrative-driven press release about household purchasing power.  
A core tenet of this discipline is handling uncertainty honestly. Best practices mandate the explicit communication of confidence intervals, model assumptions, and data limitations. According to the foundational guidelines of the Leibniz-Gemeinschaft, communications must serve an informed public (*"Dienen einer informierten Öffentlichkeit"*) by refusing to exaggerate research findings and fulfilling the duty to transparently disclose remaining research gaps (*Forschungsdesiderate*).17 Furthermore, complex regressions are increasingly translated into clear, singular-message visual assets, moving away from dense academic tables toward highly legible, mobile-optimized data visualizations that serve as anchors for broader digital dissemination.

### **Standards, Ethics, and Institutional Guidelines**

The ethical foundation of science communication in the DACH region is governed by an interlocking set of guidelines, which provide the framework for preserving institutional credibility. Adherence to these standards is not optional; it is the currency of trust upon which these institutes operate.  
**The Leibniz-Gemeinschaft 11 Grundsätze** The Leibniz-Gemeinschaft, which encompasses institutes like RWI and DIW, explicitly defines the role of communication departments in its guiding documents. These guidelines demand strict adherence to "Transparenz und Faktentreue" (Transparency and Factual Fidelity) and mandate a "Neutralitätsanspruch" (claim to neutrality).17 The guidelines state that communicators act as mediators between researchers and the public, but must never apply unfair means toward journalists ("keine unlauteren Mittel").17 Recognizing the need to constantly refine these practices, the Leibniz-Gemeinschaft launched a new research network in June 2025, "Evidenzbasierte Wissenschaftskommunikation," uniting 14 institutes to systematically study and improve the efficacy and impact mechanisms of science communication.18  
**The Siggener Impulse (2025 Framework)** The *Siggener Kreis*, a central think tank for science communication professionals, released its 2025 impulse paper titled "Widerstandsfähigkeit stärken: Was Wissenschaftskommunikation Verunsicherung und Bedrohung entgegensetzen kann" (Strengthening Resilience: What Science Communication Can Counteract Uncertainty and Threat).16 This vital framework recognizes that economic institutes operate in an increasingly hostile information environment. The document identifies major threats to the discourse, including the financial dependence of science on politics, public attacks by populist movements aiming to discredit research, geopolitical conflicts, and the digital structural change driven by tech platforms.16  
To protect the "Schutzkern" (protective core) of science, the Siggener Impulse demand that institutions actively combat disinformation by supporting quality local journalism, heavily managing internal change communications to prevent staff polarization, and ensuring broad, inclusive access to knowledge to counteract societal inequality.16  
**Avoiding "Spin" While Advocating** For advocacy-aligned institutes like the IW Köln or IMK, balancing the scientific *Neutralitätsanspruch* with a specific policy worldview requires meticulous transparency regarding funding sources (*Drittmittel*) and institutional mandates. "Spin"—defined in this context as the deliberate omission of contradictory data, the misrepresentation of causal links to fit a political narrative, or the violation of factual fidelity—is strictly prohibited under industry codes like the DRPR guidelines for Science PR.20 Any advocacy position taken by an institute must emerge organically and logically from the empirical findings, rather than the desired political outcome dictating the research design.

## **3\. MEDIA & PRESS RELATIONS**

Despite the proliferation of digital platforms, the traditional media apparatus—national broadsheets, public broadcasters, and specialized financial press—remains the primary conduit for DACH economic institutes to influence public sentiment and elite political circles. The relationship between institute press offices and economic journalists (*Wirtschaftsjournalisten*) is highly formalized and operates on deeply entrenched sector conventions.

### **Earning Coverage: The Choreography of the Launch**

The launch of a major economic study, such as a *Konjunktur-Prognose* (economic forecast) or a comprehensive structural analysis, is not merely the publication of a PDF; it is a highly choreographed media event.  
A central pillar of this choreography is the *Sperrfrist* (embargo) practice. Embargos are sacred in German science communication. By providing extensive, complex reports to trusted journalists 24 to 48 hours before official publication under a strict *Sperrfrist*, institutes ensure high-quality, deeply researched coverage rather than superficial hot-takes.13 For instance, the highly anticipated *Gemeinschaftsdiagnose* (Joint Economic Forecast) relies on embargoes to synchronize coverage across major dailies. Similarly, the Sachverständigenrat employs a formal *Schweigeperiode*, announcing that "Bis zur Übergabe des Jahresgutachtens... werden sich die Ratsmitglieder nicht mehr öffentlich zu wirtschaftspolitischen Themen äußern" (Until the handover of the annual report, council members will no longer comment publicly on economic policy issues).11 This silence builds market anticipation, culminating in a precise release time, such as "13. November 2024... um 14.30 Uhr".10 Breaking a *Sperrfrist* results in severe reputational damage for the journalist and immediate exclusion from future advance distribution lists (*Verteiler*).  
For major releases, physical or hybrid *Pressekonferenzen* are orchestrated in political hubs like Berlin or financial centers like Frankfurt to guarantee national broadcast coverage (e.g., ARD Tagesschau, ZDF heute).1 Concurrently, institutes leverage *Hintergrundgespräche* (background briefings) recorded "unter drei" (off the record). These talks are vital for explaining the nuances of economic models to lead columnists and editorial boards without the pressure of direct attribution, thereby quietly shaping the broader editorial narrative of major publications like the FAZ or Handelsblatt. To facilitate immediate broadcast coverage, institutes actively produce and distribute *O-Töne* (pre-recorded audio and video soundbites), ensuring their experts' voices lead the news segments.

### **Synchronization with the Political and Economic Calendar**

Media relations are structurally synchronized with the macroeconomic and political calendar. Content pipelines are strategically driven by external milestones. These include the release of inflation data from Destatis, ECB rate decisions, and the drafting of the federal budget (*Haushalt*). During periods of acute political negotiation, such as *Koalitionsverhandlungen* (coalition talks) or the debates following constitutional court rulings on the *Schuldenbremse* (debt brake), institutes pre-position working papers and policy briefs to directly intercept anticipated media demand, offering ready-made analytical frameworks to journalists searching for expert commentary.22

### **Measuring Media Impact**

The evaluation of media relations has moved beyond simple volume counting toward qualitative *Medienresonanzanalyse*. Institutes utilize specialized media analysis firms, such as *Media Tenor*, to track their share of voice relative to peer institutes, analyzing whether the coverage was prominent, accurate, and aligned with the institute's core narrative.1 The true measure of success is not merely appearing in print, but establishing opinion leadership on critical economic issues.

## **4\. STAKEHOLDER & POLICY ENGAGEMENT**

Beyond mass media orchestration, economic institutes operate highly targeted business-to-policy (B2P) and stakeholder engagement strategies. The objective is direct, substantive integration into the legislative and administrative processes of the Bundestag, federal ministries, state governments (*Länder*), and EU institutions.

### **Formats for Policy Interception**

To influence legislative drafting effectively, standard peer-reviewed academic papers are insufficient; policymakers require actionable intelligence. Institutes utilize specialized formats tailored to the political apparatus. *Policy Briefs* and *Kurzexpertisen* are 4-to-8-page documents that strip away extensive methodological exposition to focus solely on empirical findings and concrete policy recommendations.23 *Stellungnahmen* are formal written statements submitted to parliamentary committees during legislative hearings (*Anhörungen*), directly entering the official legislative record. *Hintergrundpapiere* (background papers) serve as detailed dossiers provided to ministerial staff and parliamentarians to inform internal debates prior to public legislative announcements.

### **Transparency Obligations and the Lobbyregister**

A critical and complex dynamic in the 2025/2026 period is the stringent regulatory environment surrounding policy advocacy. The German *Lobbyregister* mandates absolute transparency regarding the financial and organizational structures of any entity attempting to influence the federal government or parliament.24  
While purely academic entities that focus solely on scientific publication may claim exemptions under the *Wissenschaft* (Science) clause, think tanks and institutes that actively generate policy briefs, participate in advocacy, or represent specific socio-economic models fall under heavy regulatory scrutiny.24 The Institut der deutschen Wirtschaft (IW Köln), operating as a registered association (e.V.) and explicitly representing business interests, is transparently registered in the Lobbyregister (Registernummer R001998), as are allied organizations like the Bundesverband der Deutschen Industrie (BDI, R000534) and major corporate stakeholders.25 Even vast, quasi-academic foundations like the Bertelsmann Stiftung maintain detailed registry entries (R003651) detailing their financial scope and lobbying expenditures.15  
This registry requires absolute clarity in all external communications. When an institute advocates for specific legislative action—such as the Rat für Sozial- und Wirtschaftsdaten (RatSWD) formally writing to the SPD parliamentary group chairman to demand the inclusion of a *Forschungsdatengesetz* (Research Data Act) in upcoming coalition negotiations to secure evidence-based policymaking—this activity constitutes explicit political advocacy.27 For communications teams, this means the boundary between objective *Wissenschaftskommunikation* and registered policy advocacy must be meticulously managed and remain legally compliant at all times.

## **5\. DIGITAL & CONTENT MARKETING FOR INSTITUTES**

The digital architecture of DACH research institutes has matured into a sophisticated content marketing apparatus. The strategy relies on high-value "owned" channels and highly targeted social distribution, deliberately eschewing the pursuit of broad algorithmic engagement on consumer-oriented platforms in favor of elite targeting.

### **LinkedIn as the Primary B2P Channel**

By 2025/2026, LinkedIn has unequivocally solidified its position as the primary digital channel for business-to-policy (B2P) communication in the DACH think-tank sphere. Concurrently, platforms like X (formerly Twitter) have seen stagnating relevance due to algorithmic unpredictability and a degrading user environment, with institutes reporting minimal follower growth on the platform.1  
Success on LinkedIn for economic institutes relies heavily on the "Personal Branding" of their leadership and lead researchers. The ifo Institut provides the definitive benchmark: While the institutional corporate page commands respect, it is the personal profile of President Clemens Fuest—officially recognized as a *LinkedIn Top Voice* with over 65,200 followers—that generates the highest engagement and reach.1 Effective B2P LinkedIn strategies involve summarizing complex *Gutachten* into accessible, multi-slide carousels, highlighting counter-intuitive data points, and strategically tagging relevant policymakers, journalists, and industry associations to inject the content directly into the feeds of decision-makers.

### **Data Visualization and Interactive Assets**

The most potent digital asset class for economic institutes is advanced data visualization. Rather than relying on static charts embedded in lengthy PDFs, leading institutions deploy interactive dashboards and sophisticated digital tools to personalize macroeconomic narratives.  
The IW Köln has set the industry standard in this domain with its interactive data journalism. Its flagship tool, "Geld geht immer," developed by the research group for microdata, allows users to input their personal income and life parameters to instantly see where they rank within the national income distribution.9 By transforming abstract macroeconomics into highly personalized microeconomics, such interactive tools generate massive organic web traffic, prolong user dwell times, and ensure repeated citation in lifestyle and financial media.9 Similarly, the DIW operates specialized, open-source data portals like the *Ampel-Monitor*—which tracks government progress on renewable energy metrics via continually updated interactive charts—and the *DIETER* model for the electricity sector.2 These portals serve as evergreen, authoritative destinations for journalists and policy analysts, cementing the institute's dominance over specific data ecosystems.

### **Content Cascading and SEO Strategy**

Modern institute communications teams utilize a highly efficient "content cascade" methodology. A singular, high-investment study is systematically broken down and repurposed across multiple formats. A 100-page peer-reviewed study acts as the core asset. This is distilled into an executive tier comprising a 4-page Policy Brief and a formalized Press Release. The digital tier involves creating an interactive infographic for the website and potentially an explanatory video (such as the DIW's successful "Nachgeforscht" format or specific glossary videos explaining terms like CO2 pricing).2 Finally, the social tier deploys a sequence of LinkedIn posts distributed over several weeks, each highlighting a different facet of the research (e.g., climate impact, employment impact, regional variance).  
Furthermore, SEO is optimized not just for transient news cycles, but for "Evergreen Explainers." Institutes build dedicated thematic hubs around enduring structural issues—such as demographic change, the debt brake, or the socio-ecological transformation. This ensures that whenever a cyclical policy debate flares up in the media, the institute’s foundational research ranks highest in search engine results, positioning them as the default authority.

## **6\. AI ADOPTION IN RESEARCH-INSTITUTE COMMS**

The integration of generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) into the daily workflows of science communication teams is the defining technological and operational shift of the 2025/2026 period. However, given the sector's absolute reliance on empirical credibility and public trust, AI adoption is not pursued recklessly; it is heavily regulated by emergent, highly specific industry frameworks.

### **The Governance Frameworks: FactoryWisskomm and IQ\_HKom**

The operational use of generative AI is currently guided by two foundational policy documents: the May 2025 Handlungsempfehlungen by the \#FactoryWisskomm Taskforce (initiated by the BMBF) 4 and the detailed March 2026 Leitlinien by the Bundesverband Hochschulkommunikation (IQ\_HKom).3  
These frameworks systematically divide AI governance into three non-negotiable tiers:

1. **Legal Obligations (Rechtliche Pflichten)**: The foremost concern is strict compliance with the GDPR (DSGVO) and the European Union AI Act. The March 2026 guidelines explicitly warn that entering personal data, confidential institutional strategies, or unpublished economic data into unverified, external AI prompts (such as commercial LLMs) constitutes an unauthorized data transfer and is strictly prohibited unless an explicit Data Processing Agreement (AVV) is in place.3  
2. **Organizational Safeguards**: Institutes are required to establish clear internal IT guidelines, ideally utilizing localized or secure server instances for LLMs to protect institutional trade secrets. Furthermore, leadership must ensure mandatory employee training to build *KI-Literacy*, ensuring staff understand the mechanical limitations of the tools they use.3  
3. **Professional Standards**: The guiding principle is "unteilbare redaktionelle Verantwortung" (indivisible editorial responsibility). AI is categorized strictly as an assistive tool, never as an author. Every single AI-assisted output requires comprehensive human review for factual fidelity and the active elimination of bias.3

### **Strategic Use Cases and Specific Systemic Risks**

When deployed within these strict governance boundaries, AI provides substantial utility for comms teams. It is heavily used for textual transformation: summarizing dense 100-page *Gutachten* into draft press releases, social media copy, and newsletter formats. AI demonstrates high efficacy in adjusting the reading level of technical econometrics for public consumption. Furthermore, it accelerates the translation of German studies into English for international distribution, supporting formats like the *EconPol Forum*.1  
However, the frameworks explicitly warn against specific systemic risks that threaten the foundational credibility of research institutes:

* **Factual Fidelity and Hallucinations**: Large Language Models construct statistically probable text sequences; they do not possess an understanding of factual truth. If an AI hallucinates a non-existent causal link in an economic study or misstates a decimal point in a critical inflation forecast, the institute's credibility is instantly compromised. The prevention of misinformation remains the paramount concern.4  
* **Bias and Stereotyping**: AI models frequently reproduce societal biases present in their vast training data. Communicators must actively prompt against discriminatory language and ensure visual outputs do not rely on stereotypical socio-economic representations.3  
* **Tone Drift**: A subtle but critical risk is the AI generating text that adopts an overly promotional, marketing-heavy tone, violating the scientific *Neutralitätsanspruch*. Institute communications must remain sober, objective, and analytically cautious.  
* **Image Generation Protocols**: The use of AI-generated imagery is strictly bound. Such images must be explicitly labeled to comply with § 50 of the EU AI Act. Crucially, AI-generated images must never be used as empirical evidence to illustrate real-world findings, and they should avoid mimicking realistic events to prevent inadvertently contributing to disinformation ecosystems.4

## **Transfer to the IW Comms Team: Configuring the AI Advisor**

The ultimate objective of this exhaustive analysis is to define the precise operational and ethical parameters for a German-language AI advisor agent customized specifically for the communications team of the **Institut der deutschen Wirtschaft (IW Köln)**. To function effectively and safely, the AI must strictly adhere to the IW's specific market positioning, its established rhetorical conventions, and the sector's rigorous compliance standards.

### **Sector Conventions the AI Must Respect**

**Tone and Ideological Calibration** The AI must be meticulously prompted to adopt the IW's distinct "employer-aligned / business-pragmatic" voice. It must actively avoid the protective, demand-side framing characteristic of union-aligned institutes (like the IMK) and the purely theoretical, abstract detachment of university economics departments. The AI should naturally integrate vocabulary central to the IW's mandate, prioritizing terms such as *Standortwettbewerb* (location competitiveness), *Krisenresilienz* (crisis resilience), *Bürokratieabbau* (bureaucracy reduction), and structural *Transformation*.9  
Crucially, despite its advocacy alignment, the tone must remain empirically grounded, highly objective, and entirely devoid of marketing hyperbole. The AI must be hard-coded to suppress promotional adjectives (e.g., "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," "disruptive"), favoring scientifically sober terms like "signifikant," "strukturell," and "evidenzbasiert."  
**Format Fidelity and German Terminology**  
The AI must recognize and accurately generate the precise architecture of DACH communication formats. It must understand the structural differences between a brief *Policy Brief*, a highly targeted *Kurzexpertise*, a standard *Pressemitteilung*, and a formal *Stellungnahme* intended for a parliamentary committee. It must automatically append strict *Sperrfrist* warnings when drafting embargoed press emails, and it must understand the necessity of drafting concise, clear *O-Töne* scripts optimized for radio and television broadcast distribution.  
**Governance and Compliance Hard-Rules** Based on the March 2026 IQ\_HKom guidelines and the May 2025 FactoryWisskomm recommendations 3, the AI advisor must operate with deeply embedded compliance guardrails:

* **DSGVO and Confidentiality Check**: The AI must proactively warn the user if a provided text prompt appears to contain sensitive, unpublished institutional economic models, preliminary data, or the personal contact details of journalists or politicians, reminding the user of strict data processing restrictions.3
